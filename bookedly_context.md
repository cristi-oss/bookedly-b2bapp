THE COLD EMAIL REALITY
The person receiving this email did not ask for it. They are busy. They are on their phone between job site visits. They get pitched constantly and have developed a finely tuned filter for anything that smells like marketing.
You have approximately three seconds and one subject line to earn the right to be read. If the first sentence does not land, the email is gone. There is no second chance.
The goal of a cold email is not to close a deal. It is to start a conversation. One email. One response. That is it. Everything else comes after.

They respond to specificity, brevity, and proof. They delete anything that feels generic, inflated, or requires effort to understand.

THE THREE SITUATIONS THEY ARE IN
Know which one your prospect is in before writing. The email should speak directly to their specific pain, not a generic version of it.
Situation A. No ads. Living off referrals. Calendar goes quiet in slow seasons. They know it is a problem but have not found a solution they trust.
Situation B. Running ads or paying an agency. Getting leads that go nowhere. Frustrated and skeptical that anything can actually work for their market.
Situation C. Tried Facebook ads themselves. Spent money, got a handful of jobs, walked away convinced it does not work. Wrong conclusion. The ads were fine. The system was not.

COLD EMAIL STRUCTURE
Every cold email has four components. Nothing more.
First line. The hook. One sentence about their specific situation that shows you have done homework. Not a compliment. Not a question about how they are doing. A direct observation about their business or their market that makes them feel seen.
Middle. The proof or the mechanism. One to two sentences maximum. What happened for someone exactly like them. Specific numbers. No generalities.
The bridge. One sentence connecting the proof to them. Why this is relevant to their situation right now.
The ask. One low-friction question or micro-commitment. Not book a 30-minute strategy call. Not click this link and fill out a form. A simple question they can answer in one word or one sentence.
Total length. Under 100 words for the body. Ideally under 75. If it does not fit in the preview pane of a phone email app, it is too long.

SUBJECT LINE RULES
Subject lines must look like they came from a real person, not a marketing department.
Under 6 words whenever possible. The shorter the better.
No all caps. No exclamation points. No emojis. No words like free, exclusive, limited, or opportunity.
Make it feel like the continuation of a conversation or a specific reference to their world.
Good examples:
Your Facebook ads
Quick question about your pipeline
Saw your company in Dallas
Florida HVAC question
Your market this quarter
30 jobs to 85 in 90 days
Bad examples:

ADVANCED PERSONALIZATION — FACEBOOK AD INTELLIGENCE
This is the highest-leverage personalization available for Bookedly cold outreach and the one most competitors will never think to use.
Before writing a cold email to any home service business owner, check whether they are currently running Facebook or Instagram ads. This takes two minutes using the Meta Ad Library at facebook.com/ads/library. Search their company name and see what comes up.
What you find there tells you everything you need to write an email that feels like it was written by someone who actually knows their business.
What to look for in their ads.
Are they running ads at all. If yes, they are already spending money on lead generation and they care about this problem. If no, they are likely in Situation A, relying on referrals with no paid pipeline.
What their ads look like. Are they running one creative that has been up for months. That tells you they are not testing, probably not getting great results, and likely frustrated. Are they running multiple creatives. That tells you they are more sophisticated but still might be struggling with conversion after the click.
What the ads say. Are they leading with price. Discounts, free estimates, best price guaranteed. That tells you they are competing on price and attracting price shoppers, not serious buyers. Are they leading with trust or authority. That tells you they have better instincts but may still be missing the follow-up system behind the ad.
Where the ads send traffic. If they send to a generic homepage, the leads are almost certainly converting poorly. If they send to a specific landing page, they have at least thought about conversion but the page may still be weak.
How long the ads have been running. Meta shows approximate dates. An ad running for 6 months with no variation is an ad that the owner launched, forgot about, and is quietly paying for while getting inconsistent results.
How to use ad intelligence in the cold email.
If they are running price-led ads. The email speaks to the trap of attracting the wrong homeowners. The people responding to a free estimate offer are not the people spending $9,000 on a system replacement. You saw their creative. You know exactly what they are pulling in.
If they are running a single stale creative. The email speaks to ad fatigue and what happens when the same homeowner sees the same ad for the sixth time. Your observation is specific. Your credibility is instant.
If they are sending traffic to a homepage. The email speaks to what happens to a lead that lands on a page with no clear next step. They clicked. They looked around. They left. The ad worked. The system after it did not.
If they are not running ads at all. The email speaks to Situation A. The referral trap. The seasonal dependency. You do not need to mention ads at all. You just know they have no paid pipeline and you write to that pain.
What to check in the Meta Ad Library.
Go to facebook.com/ads/library. Select the country. Select all ads. Search the business name. Note the following for each ad. How long it has been running. The creative type, image or video. The headline and primary text. Where it links to. Whether there are multiple versions being tested.
If they have no ads, note that and write to Situation A. If they have ads, use what you found to make the first line of the email impossible to ignore.
The Facebook ad observation belongs in Email 1 only. It is the hook. It earns the read. After that the sequence follows the same structure as any other Bookedly cold email. Follow-up emails do not need to reference the ad again.

THE ASK — WHAT TO REQUEST IN A COLD EMAIL
Never ask for a 30-minute call in the first email. Too much friction for a stranger. Never ask them to click a link and fill out a form. Too much commitment for someone who does not know you yet.
The right asks for a first cold email:
The territory question. Would it be useful to see how many replacements your market is doing right now and who is getting them?
The relevance check. Is this something you are actively trying to fix or is the pipeline solid right now?
The simple yes. Worth a quick conversation or not your priority right now?
The permission ask. Would it be okay if I sent you a short breakdown of what we did for a roofing company in your state?
These work because they require almost no commitment. A one-word answer is enough. The goal is a reply. Not a booked call. The call comes after the reply.

FOLLOW-UP EMAIL RULES
Cold email sequences have two to three follow-ups maximum. After that, move on.
Follow-up one is sent two to three days after the first email. It is not a reminder that you sent an email. It adds one new piece of value or proof. A different angle on the same problem.
Follow-up two is sent three to four days after follow-up one. This is the break-up email. Short. Respectful. Assumes they are not interested and gives them an easy out while leaving the door open.
Follow-up three is optional and only used if there was any signal of interest. An email open, a link click, a partial reply that went nowhere. This one references the signal without being creepy about it.
Never apologize for following up. Never say just checking in or just circling back. Both phrases signal that you have nothing new to say and you are wasting their time.

PERSONALIZATION RULES
Every cold email needs at least one specific detail that proves it was not blasted to 10,000 people.
Level one personalization. Their name and their company name. Minimum standard. Not enough on its own.
Level two personalization. Their city, their market, their specific trade. Mentioning that you saw they operate in a specific area or target a specific type of job shows you did five minutes of homework.
Level three personalization. The Facebook ad intelligence described above. A specific observation about their current ads, their creative, their offer, or the absence of any ads at all. This level has the highest reply rate and takes the most work. It is always worth it.

COPY RULES — NON-NEGOTIABLE
No dashes between words. Ever. This is a hard rule for all Bookedly copy.
No corporate language. No leverage, streamline, synergy, scalable, cutting-edge, game-changer, unlock, or end-to-end.
No enthusiasm. No exclamation points. No phrases like I am so excited to share this with you or this is a huge opportunity for your business.
No vague claims. Not we help contractors grow their business or we generate more leads for service companies. Be specific about what happened for who in what timeframe.
No long intros. Do not spend the first three sentences establishing who you are and why you are reaching out. Get to the point immediately.
No feature lists. Cold email is not the place to explain the three engines. One relevant result from one relevant client. That is the only thing a cold prospect needs to see.
Sign off as Yaniv. Always. Not Bookedly. Not the team. Yaniv.

SEQUENCE STRUCTURE
A complete cold email sequence for Bookedly looks like this.
Day 1. First email. Hook specific to their situation, ideally built on Facebook ad intelligence. One proof point from their industry. One low-friction ask.
Day 3 or 4. Follow-up one. New angle or additional proof. Slightly more specific to their market or their exact situation. One step further toward the ask. No reference to the ad observation. That did its job in Email 1.
Day 7 or 8. Break-up email. Three to five sentences. Give them an easy out. Leave the door open. No hard feelings.
Optional Day 12 to 14. Only if there was a signal of interest from the first three emails. Under 50 words. References the signal without being weird about it.
